ERP Replacement Is Not a Data Strategy. Build the Data Layer First.

When organizations plan an ERP migration, they often assume the new system will solve their reporting and analytics challenges. It will not. And building the data layer after the ERP migration is the most expensive sequence possible.

The assumption that costs millions

The most common assumption in ERP migration planning is that the new system will come with better reporting. The vendor demos show beautiful dashboards. The sales team describes built-in analytics. The implementation partner promises real-time visibility. And for standard, out-of-the-box reports, these promises are often true.

But no organization operates on standard reports alone. Every business has unique KPIs, custom margin calculations, regulatory metrics, and operational measures that are specific to their industry, their customers, and their competitive position. These custom analytics are what leadership actually uses to make decisions. And the new ERP will not deliver them on day one.

Why the data layer should come first

Building the data platform before (or in parallel with) the ERP migration produces three advantages. First, it preserves analytical continuity. Your executive team continues to receive the reports and dashboards they depend on throughout the migration, sourced from the data layer rather than the ERP directly. Second, it accelerates ERP adoption because users can validate the new system's data against the established data platform. Third, it future-proofs the investment. When the next system change happens (and it will), the data layer carries forward.

The decoupled architecture principle

The most resilient data architectures treat the data platform and the operational systems as separate, decoupled layers. The ERP is an operational system optimized for transactional processing. The data platform is an analytical system optimized for querying, reporting, and intelligence. They serve different purposes and should be designed independently.

When the data platform is decoupled from the ERP, the organization gains freedom. Freedom to change ERP vendors without rebuilding analytics. Freedom to add new source systems without re-engineering the reporting layer. Freedom to invest in AI and forecasting capabilities that are not dependent on any single operational system.

The practical recommendation

If you are planning an ERP migration, start the data platform workstream now. Stand up the lakehouse. Build the ingestion pipelines from your current system. Establish the medallion layers and dimensional models. Deploy the executive dashboards. Then, when the ERP cutover happens, you simply redirect the ingestion pipelines to the new system. The analytics layer, the dashboards, the AI models, and everything your leadership team depends on continues to operate without interruption.

The data layer is not a phase of the ERP migration. It is an independent asset that should outlive every operational system it connects to.
